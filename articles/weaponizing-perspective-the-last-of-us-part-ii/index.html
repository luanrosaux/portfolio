Weaponizing Perspective in The Last of Us Part II

How a game can use control, repetition and character switching to make empathy happen through the player's hands.

22
Source
O Meio Noob video essay
Format
Video-to-article adaptation
Topic
Game UX and narrative agency

key ideas

  • The game uses perspective as a design tool, not only as a storytelling device.
  • Empathy in games can be built through interaction, repetition and survival loops.
  • The Abby switch works because the controls stay familiar while the meaning changes.
  • Discomfort can be intentional UX when it serves the emotional goal of the experience.

The Last of Us Part II does something unusually aggressive with perspective. It does not simply ask the player to understand another side of the story. It hands that side to the player, places it inside the same interaction model, and says: survive.

About this adaptation

This article is an English editorial adaptation inspired by the O Meio Noob video O jogo te traiu: como The Last of Us Part II manipulou sua empatia. It is not a literal transcript. The goal is to translate the video's Game UX argument into a portfolio-friendly written analysis for an international audience.

The game turns perspective into a system

Many stories change perspective. A film can cut to another character. A book can change narrator. A series can dedicate an episode to the antagonist. But games have a different weapon: they can make the player operate the other perspective.

That distinction matters. The Last of Us Part II does not only show Abby's side after the player has learned to hate her. It turns Abby into a playable body with resources, upgrades, animation, vulnerability and risk. The player is no longer observing an argument for empathy. The player is performing it.

This is why the shift feels so invasive. The game does not ask for permission before changing the emotional contract. It uses the controller as the bridge between rejection and understanding.

Perspective becomes playable The player does not only see Abby differently. The player learns Abby through use.

Mechanical empathy

Empathy in cinema often comes from watching. You see someone suffer, lose, hesitate or try again. In games, empathy can also come from maintenance. You heal a character. You manage their ammunition. You look for supplies. You fail with them, adapt with them and protect them from danger.

That is the uncomfortable genius of Abby's section. At first, she may still be the person who destroyed the player's emotional center. But then she becomes a character with a rhythm. She has weight. She has a different physicality. Her combat communicates force. Her movement communicates presence.

Little by little, the player's hands begin to know things that the player's head may not want to accept: Abby can die, Abby can be cornered, Abby can protect someone, Abby can be afraid, Abby can need you.

The reset that hurts

The most fragile part of this design is the beginning of Abby's campaign. Narratively, the game needs to rebuild a rejected character from zero. Mechanically, it also resets part of the progression. Emotionally, the player may not want to be there at all.

This creates a risky collision. The player has just left a dramatic peak and may want resolution, confrontation and catharsis. Instead, the game responds with another beginning: new spaces, new relationships, new routines and another learning curve.

As a design decision, that is brave. As a user experience, it is dangerous. The concept is strong, but the early loop carries too much emotional resistance. A slightly faster entrance into Abby's mechanical identity could protect the moment without softening its discomfort.

Familiar controls, unfamiliar meaning

One reason the transition works is that the game does not become a different game. The interface remains clear. The verbs remain familiar. You still scavenge, craft, aim, hide, listen, heal and upgrade. The model of play stays coherent.

That continuity is essential. If the controls became confusing at the same time that the narrative became emotionally hostile, the player could reject the experience for technical reasons. Instead, the discomfort comes from meaning, not from usability failure.

This is subtle Game UX work. Abby must feel different enough to be a new body, but familiar enough that the player can keep moving. The change happens in interpretation. The manual does not need to be rewritten.

When Ellie becomes the threat

The theater confrontation is one of the clearest examples of weaponized perspective. Ellie, who was the emotional center of the player's experience, becomes the danger in the room. The player knows her brutality because the player helped perform it.

In that moment, familiarity turns into fear. Ellie is not just an enemy. She is the memory of a previous agency returning against the player. The game makes you survive against someone you have already learned to protect.

That is more than a plot twist. It is an interaction twist. The game changes the emotional meaning of the same verbs by changing who those verbs are protecting.

Discomfort as UX, not a UX mistake

The Last of Us Part II remains divisive because it manipulates something players often expect to control: identification. We are used to deciding who we care about, who we protect and where our loyalty sits.

This game makes that process unstable. It takes the player's attachment to Ellie, uses it to generate anger, then places that anger inside another playable body. It does not erase Ellie's pain or sanitize Abby's violence. It makes both perspectives playable enough that the player loses the comfort of a clean moral distance.

That is not always pleasant. But it is designed. Sometimes a well-designed experience is not the one that makes the user comfortable. Sometimes it is the one that knows exactly when to remove comfort from the system.

takeaway

The perspective is not just told. It is played.
